# 1.2 Hybridization

> [!tip] One Line
> Hybridization = mixing of atomic orbitals of similar energies to form new, equivalent hybrid orbitals before bonding.

---

## Why Hybridization Exists — The Problem

Carbon's ground state configuration: **1s² 2s² 2p¹ 2p¹**
- Only 2 unpaired electrons → should form only 2 bonds
- But carbon forms 4 bonds (methane CH₄ has 4 identical C-H bonds)
- Also, if bonds came from different orbitals (s and p), they'd be different lengths and angles — but in methane all 4 bonds are **identical**

**Solution:** Before bonding, carbon **promotes** one electron and **mixes** its orbitals:

```
Ground state:        1s²  2s²  2px¹  2py¹  2pz⁰
                              ↑↑    ↑     
Excited state:       1s²  2s¹  2px¹  2py¹  2pz¹
                          ↑    ↑     ↑     ↑
Then hybridize → new equivalent orbitals
```

---

## sp³ Hybridization

**1s + 3p orbitals mix → 4 sp³ hybrid orbitals**

### Properties
- 4 identical orbitals, pointing to corners of a **tetrahedron**
- Bond angle: **109.5°**
- All bonds are **sigma (σ) bonds** — single bonds only
- No unhybridized p orbital left → no pi bonds possible

### Shape
```
        H
        |  109.5°
    H — C — H
        |
        H
     Methane (CH₄)
```

### Examples
- All **alkanes** (CH₄, C₂H₆, C₃H₈...)
- Alcohols (C-O in sp³)
- Amines (N can be sp³)

> [!note] 
> In sp³: 25% s-character, 75% p-character

---

## sp² Hybridization

**1s + 2p orbitals mix → 3 sp² hybrid orbitals**
**1 unhybridized p orbital remains (perpendicular to the plane)**

### Properties
- 3 hybrid orbitals in a **trigonal planar** arrangement
- Bond angle: **120°**
- The leftover p orbital forms a **pi (π) bond** by sideways overlap
- One double bond = **1σ + 1π**

### Shape
```
    H       H
     \     /
      C = C        Ethene (C₂H₄)
     /     \
    H       H
    Bond angle = 120°
    All atoms in one plane
```

### Why No Free Rotation in Double Bonds?
- The π bond is formed by sideways overlap of p orbitals
- Rotation would break this overlap → very high energy needed
- Therefore **double bonds are rigid** — leads to geometric (cis/trans) isomerism

### Examples
- All **alkenes** (C=C)
- **Benzene** (aromatic ring)
- Aldehydes, Ketones (C=O carbon is sp²)
- Carboxylic acids (C=O carbon is sp²)

> [!note]
> In sp²: 33% s-character, 67% p-character

---

## sp Hybridization

**1s + 1p orbital mix → 2 sp hybrid orbitals**
**2 unhybridized p orbitals remain**

### Properties
- 2 hybrid orbitals at **180°** — linear shape
- The 2 leftover p orbitals form **2 pi bonds**
- Triple bond = **1σ + 2π**

### Shape
```
H — C ≡ C — H        Ethyne (C₂H₂)
    180°
    Linear
```

### Examples
- All **alkynes** (C≡C)
- CO₂ (O=C=O, carbon is sp)
- HCN (H-C≡N, carbon is sp)

> [!note]
> In sp: 50% s-character, 50% p-character

---

## Sigma vs Pi Bonds — Critical Distinction

| Feature | Sigma (σ) bond | Pi (π) bond |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Overlap | Head-on (end-to-end) | Sideways (parallel p orbitals) |
| Present in | ALL bonds (single, double, triple) | Only double & triple bonds |
| Rotation | Free rotation possible | NO free rotation |
| Strength | Stronger (direct overlap) | Weaker (indirect overlap) |
| Count in bonds | Single = 1σ, Double = 1σ+1π, Triple = 1σ+2π | |

> [!important] Exam Favourite
> Q: How many σ and π bonds in CH₂=CH–C≡CH?
> - CH₂=CH: 1π bond (the double bond)
> - C≡CH: 2π bonds (the triple bond)
> - All C-C and C-H single bonds: σ bonds
> Count all bonds: 9σ + 3π

## Comparison Table

| Property | sp³ | sp² | sp |
|----------|-----|-----|----|
| Orbitals mixed | 1s + 3p | 1s + 2p | 1s + 1p |
| Hybrid orbitals formed | 4 | 3 | 2 |
| Unhybridized p orbitals | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| Bond angle | 109.5° | 120° | 180° |
| Shape | Tetrahedral | Trigonal planar | Linear |
| π bonds possible | No | 1 | 2 |
| s-character | 25% | 33% | 50% |
| Example | CH₄, alkanes | C₂H₄, benzene | C₂H₂, alkynes |

## Effect of s-Character on Properties

More s-character → electrons held closer to nucleus → more electronegative carbon → **shorter, stronger bonds**

| Bond | Hybridization | Bond Length | Bond Strength |
|------|--------------|-------------|---------------|
| C(sp³)–H | sp³ | 109 pm | 413 kJ/mol |
| C(sp²)–H | sp² | 108 pm | 444 kJ/mol |
| C(sp)–H | sp | 106 pm | 506 kJ/mol |
| C–C (sp³-sp³) | sp³ | 154 pm | 347 kJ/mol |
| C=C (sp²-sp²) | sp² | 134 pm | 614 kJ/mol |
| C≡C (sp-sp) | sp | 120 pm | 839 kJ/mol |

> [!important] Pattern to Remember
> Bond length: sp³ > sp² > sp (longer → shorter)
> Bond strength: sp³ < sp² < sp (weaker → stronger)

---

## Acidity and s-Character (Competitive Exam)

More s-character → carbon holds electrons more tightly → conjugate base (carbanion) is more stable → **more acidic**

```
Acidity order:
Alkyne (sp, C-H) > Alkene (sp², C-H) > Alkane (sp³, C-H)
HC≡CH > H₂C=CH₂ > CH₃-CH₃
pKa:   26      44          50

→ Terminal alkynes are surprisingly acidic for hydrocarbons
→ This is why they react with NaNH₂ (a strong base) to form acetylide ions
```

## Practice Questions

1. How many σ and π bonds are in: `CH₃–CH=CH–C≡C–CH₃`?

2. The bond angle in ethyne (C₂H₂) is _______.

3. Why can't sp³ carbon form a pi bond?

4. Arrange in order of increasing bond length: C≡C, C–C, C=C

5. Why are sp hybridized carbons more acidic than sp³?

6. Which hybridization state has the maximum s-character?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. Count: all single bonds = σ, C=C gives 1π, C≡C gives 2π. Total: 11σ + 3π
> 2. 180°
> 3. sp³ has no unhybridized p orbitals left; π bonds require sideways overlap of p orbitals
> 4. C≡C (120pm) < C=C (134pm) < C–C (154pm)
> 5. Higher s-character = electrons held closer to nucleus = more electronegative carbon = better stabilizes the negative charge in carbanion = more acidic
> 6. sp (50% s-character)
