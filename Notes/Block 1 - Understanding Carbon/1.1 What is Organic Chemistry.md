# 1.1 What is Organic Chemistry

> [!tip] One Line
> Organic chemistry = study of carbon compounds (except CO, CO₂, carbonates, bicarbonates, cyanides as convention)

---

## The Origin — Vital Force Theory

- **1815 — Berzelius** proposed that organic compounds can only be made by living organisms, through a mysterious "vital force"
- This meant: no lab synthesis of organic compounds was possible
- **1828 — Friedrich Wöhler** destroyed this theory

### Wöhler's Experiment
```
NH₄CNO  ──heat──→  NH₂CONH₂
Ammonium cyanate       Urea
(inorganic)          (organic — found in urine)
```

> [!important] Why this matters
> Wöhler proved organic compounds can be made from inorganic starting materials in a lab. This opened the door to synthetic chemistry — medicines, plastics, dyes, everything.

---

## Why Carbon is the Basis of Life

Carbon is element number 6. Its electronic configuration: **2, 4**
- 2 electrons in first shell
- 4 electrons in second shell (valence shell)

This gives carbon **4 valence electrons** → it needs 4 more to complete its octet → forms **4 covalent bonds**.

### The 4 Special Properties of Carbon

**1. Tetravalency**
Carbon always forms 4 bonds. Always. No exceptions in organic chemistry.
```
     H
     |
 H — C — H     Methane: carbon bonded to 4 hydrogens
     |
     H
```

**2. Catenation**
Carbon bonds with other carbon atoms to form chains of any length.
```
C–C–C–C–C–C–C–C  (octane — 8 carbons, in petrol)
```
No other element does this to the same extent.
- Silicon (Si) can form Si-Si bonds but they are weaker and break in water
- Sulfur forms S-S bonds but only short chains (max 8)
- Carbon chains can have thousands of atoms (e.g., DNA, proteins)

**3. Multiple Bonding**
Carbon can form single (C–C), double (C=C), and triple (C≡C) bonds.
This creates huge structural variety from the same atoms.

**4. Small Atomic Size = Strong Bonds**
- C–C bond energy = 347 kJ/mol (very stable)
- C–H bond energy = 414 kJ/mol (very stable)
- These bonds don't break easily → stable compounds

> [!note] Competitive Exam Point
> Why doesn't silicon replace carbon in life? Two reasons:
> 1. Si-Si bonds are weaker (226 kJ/mol vs 347 kJ/mol for C-C)
> 2. Si forms SiO₂ (solid, rigid — like glass/sand) while C forms CO₂ (gas — easily released/absorbed by organisms)

## Classification of Carbon Atoms

Based on how many **other carbons** a carbon atom is directly bonded to:

| Type | Bonded to how many C? | Has how many H? |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Primary (1°) | 1 carbon | 3 H (if saturated) |
| Secondary (2°) | 2 carbons | 2 H (if saturated) |
| Tertiary (3°) | 3 carbons | 1 H (if saturated) |
| Quaternary (4°) | 4 carbons | 0 H |

### Example: 2-methylbutane
```
        CH₃
         |
CH₃ — CH — CH₂ — CH₃
 (a)  (b)   (c)   (d)
```
- Carbon (a): bonded to 1 C → **Primary**
- Carbon (b): bonded to 3 C (a, CH₃ branch, c) → **Tertiary**
- Carbon (c): bonded to 2 C (b and d) → **Secondary**
- Carbon (d): bonded to 1 C → **Primary**
- The branch CH₃: bonded to 1 C → **Primary**

> [!warning] Common Mistake
> When classifying carbon type, count only carbon-carbon bonds, not C-H bonds.

## Types of Carbon Compounds

### By Chain Structure

**Open Chain (Acyclic)**
```
CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃    straight chain (n-butane)

      CH₃
       |
CH₃–CH–CH₃           branched chain (isobutane)
```

**Closed Chain (Cyclic)**
- **Alicyclic** — ring of carbons, behaves like open-chain compounds
  ```
  Cyclohexane: 6 carbons in a ring, no double bonds
  ```
- **Aromatic** — ring with special delocalized electrons (benzene and its derivatives)
  ```
  Benzene: 6 carbons, alternating double bonds, extra stability
  ```

### By Functional Group
Will be covered in detail in Block 2. But the idea: same carbon skeleton, different group attached = completely different properties.

## Isomerism Preview

Same molecular formula → different structural arrangements → different compounds.

**C₄H₁₀ has 2 isomers:**
```
n-butane:   CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃       bp = -0.5°C

isobutane:  CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH₃       bp = -11.7°C
```
Same formula. Different structure. Different boiling points.

This will be explored deeply in the Isomerism block.
