# 1.3 Bond Properties

> [!tip] One Line
> Bond length, bond angle, and bond energy are all determined by hybridization — more s-character = shorter, stronger bonds.

---

## Bond Length

Distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms.

| Bond | Length |
|------|--------|
| C–C (sp³–sp³) | 154 pm |
| C=C (sp²–sp²) | 134 pm |
| C≡C (sp–sp) | 120 pm |
| C–H (sp³) | 109 pm |
| C–H (sp²) | 108 pm |
| C–H (sp) | 106 pm |
| C–O | 143 pm |
| C=O | 122 pm |
| C–N | 147 pm |
| C–Cl | 177 pm |

**Rule:** More bonds between atoms → shorter bond length
**Rule:** More s-character → shorter bond length

---

## Bond Energy (Bond Dissociation Energy)

Energy required to break one mole of a bond homolytically (each atom gets one electron).

| Bond | Energy (kJ/mol) |
|------|-----------------|
| C–H | 413 |
| C–C | 347 |
| C=C | 614 |
| C≡C | 839 |
| C–O | 358 |
| C=O | 745 |
| C–N | 305 |
| C–Cl | 339 |
| O–H | 463 |
| N–H | 391 |

**Rule:** More bonds → more energy to break (but NOT proportional — double bond ≠ 2× single bond)
- C–C: 347, C=C: 614 (not 694), C≡C: 839 (not 1041)
- The π bonds are **weaker than σ bonds** — that's why alkenes/alkynes are reactive

> [!important] 
> The π bond in C=C is only 614–347 = **267 kJ/mol** strong (much weaker than σ)
> This is why addition reactions happen at double bonds — easier to break the π bond

---

## Bond Angle

Angle between two bonds at the same atom.

| Hybridization | Ideal angle | Example | Actual angle |
|---------------|-------------|---------|--------------|
| sp³ | 109.5° | CH₄ | 109.5° |
| sp³ (with lone pair) | <109.5° | NH₃ | 107° |
| sp³ (with 2 lone pairs) | <<109.5° | H₂O | 104.5° |
| sp² | 120° | C₂H₄ | 120° |
| sp | 180° | C₂H₂ | 180° |

### VSEPR Effect on Bond Angle
Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs → compress bond angles

```
CH₄:  109.5°  (4 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs)
NH₃:  107°    (3 bonding pairs, 1 lone pair)
H₂O:  104.5°  (2 bonding pairs, 2 lone pairs)
```

---

## Bond Polarity

When two different atoms share a bond, the more electronegative atom pulls electrons toward itself → **polar bond**.

**Electronegativity order:** F > O > N > Cl > Br > C > H

- C–H bond: nearly non-polar (C and H have similar electronegativity)
- C–O, C–N, C–Cl, C–F: polar bonds
- The δ+ and δ- created by polarity determines how reactions happen

> [!note] Competitive Exam
> Bond polarity leads to the inductive effect — we cover this in Block 3, but it all starts here.

## Practice Questions

1. Arrange in order of increasing bond energy: C–C, C=C, C≡C

2. The bond angle in water (104.5°) is less than methane (109.5°). Why?

3. Which is shorter: C–O or C=O? Why?

4. Why is the π bond in ethylene weaker than the σ bond?

5. C–H bond in acetylene (sp) vs methane (sp³): which is stronger and why?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. C–C (347) < C=C (614) < C≡C (839)
> 2. Water has 2 lone pairs that occupy more space and compress the H-O-H angle below the ideal 109.5°
> 3. C=O is shorter (122 pm vs 143 pm) — double bond pulls nuclei closer
> 4. π bond is sideways overlap (less effective) vs head-on overlap for σ; π bond energy ≈ 267 kJ/mol, σ C-C ≈ 347 kJ/mol
> 5. sp (acetylene) C–H is stronger (506 kJ/mol) because 50% s-character holds electrons closer to nucleus
