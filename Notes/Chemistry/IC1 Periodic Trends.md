# IC1 Periodic Trends & Chemical Bonding

> [!tip] One Line
> Across period: atomic radius ↓, IE ↑, EN ↑. Down group: radius ↑, IE ↓. Bonding: VSEPR gives shape, hybridization gives angles.

---

## Periodic Trends

### Atomic Radius
- **Across period (→):** decreases (more protons, same shell, electrons pulled in)
- **Down group (↓):** increases (new shells added)

### Ionization Energy (IE)
- **Across period (→):** generally increases (harder to remove electron from smaller, more nuclear-charged atom)
- **Exceptions:** IE(Be) > IE(B) (B has lone 2p electron, easier to remove); IE(N) > IE(O) (N has half-filled 2p, extra stable)
- **Down group (↓):** decreases

### Electron Affinity (EA)
- Generally increases across period (more nuclear charge, wants electron more)
- Cl has highest EA (not F — F is small, electron-electron repulsion in compact 2p)

### Electronegativity (EN)
- Across period: increases; Down group: decreases
- Highest: F (3.98); Lowest: Cs/Fr

## Chemical Bonding

### VSEPR Theory
Shape depends on electron pairs around central atom (bonding + lone pairs).

| BP | LP | Shape | Example | Angle |
|----|-----|-------|---------|-------|
| 2 | 0 | Linear | BeCl₂, CO₂ | 180° |
| 3 | 0 | Trigonal planar | BF₃ | 120° |
| 2 | 1 | Bent/V-shape | SO₂ | <120° |
| 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | CH₄ | 109.5° |
| 3 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | NH₃ | 107° |
| 2 | 2 | Bent | H₂O | 104.5° |
| 5 | 0 | Trigonal bipyramidal | PCl₅ | 90°, 120° |
| 6 | 0 | Octahedral | SF₆ | 90° |

**Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs → angles decrease**

### Hybridization Summary
| Hybridization | Shape | Bond angle | Example |
|--------------|-------|-----------|---------|
| sp | Linear | 180° | BeCl₂, C₂H₂, CO₂ |
| sp² | Trigonal planar | 120° | BF₃, C₂H₄, SO₃ |
| sp³ | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | CH₄, NH₃, H₂O |
| sp³d | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°/120° | PCl₅ |
| sp³d² | Octahedral | 90° | SF₆ |

## Molecular Orbital Theory (Basics)

Orbitals combine to form bonding (σ, π) and antibonding (σ*, π*) MOs.

**Bond order = (bonding e⁻ – antibonding e⁻) / 2**

| Molecule | Bond Order | Magnetic |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| H₂ | 1 | Diamagnetic |
| He₂ | 0 (doesn't exist) | — |
| N₂ | 3 | Diamagnetic |
| O₂ | 2 | Paramagnetic (2 unpaired e⁻ in π*) |
| F₂ | 1 | Diamagnetic |

## Hydrogen Bonding

Forms between H (bonded to F, O, N) and lone pair on F, O, N.

**Effects:**
- Raises boiling point (H₂O > H₂S; HF > HCl)
- Responsible for unusual properties of water (high specific heat, surface tension, ice less dense)
- Intramolecular H-bond in o-nitrophenol → lower BP than p-nitrophenol

---

## Dipole Moment

μ = q × d (charge × distance). Unit: Debye (D)

- Symmetric molecules have μ = 0: CO₂, BF₃, CCl₄, PCl₅, SF₆
- Asymmetric or with lone pairs: μ ≠ 0: HCl, H₂O, NH₃, CHCl₃

## Practice Questions
1. Why is IE of N > O despite O having more protons?
2. Bond angle: NH₃ vs H₂O — which is larger and why?
3. Why does O₂ show paramagnetism?
4. Which has μ = 0: CCl₄, CHCl₃, H₂O, CO₂?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. N has half-filled 2p (2p³) — extra stability. O has one paired electron in 2p⁴, repulsion makes it easier to remove
> 2. NH₃ (107°) > H₂O (104.5°) — water has 2 lone pairs (more repulsion) vs NH₃'s 1 lone pair
> 3. O₂ MO configuration has 2 electrons in degenerate π* orbitals (Hund's rule) → 2 unpaired → paramagnetic
> 4. CCl₄ (tetrahedral, symmetric) and CO₂ (linear, symmetric) have μ = 0
