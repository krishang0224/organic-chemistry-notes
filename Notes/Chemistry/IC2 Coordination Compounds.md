# IC2 Coordination Compounds

> [!tip] One Line
> Central metal + ligands = complex. IUPAC name: ligands alphabetically first, then metal, then anion. Crystal field splits d orbitals.

---

## Key Definitions

- **Central metal atom/ion:** transition metal accepting lone pairs
- **Ligand:** electron pair donor (Lewis base) bonded to metal
- **Coordination number (CN):** number of donor atoms bonded to metal
- **Coordination sphere:** metal + ligands inside [ ]
- **Chelate:** complex with ligand forming ring (e.g., EDTA, en)

---

## Types of Ligands

| Type | Donor atoms | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Monodentate | 1 | Cl⁻, NH₃, H₂O, CN⁻, NO₂⁻ |
| Bidentate | 2 | en (ethylenediamine), C₂O₄²⁻ (oxalate) |
| Polydentate | 4–6 | EDTA (hexadentate — forms 5 rings) |
| Ambidentate | can donate via either of 2 atoms | NO₂⁻ (via N or O), SCN⁻ |

**Chelate effect:** polydentate ligands form more stable complexes (entropy increase).

---

## IUPAC Nomenclature

**Rules:**
1. Cation named before anion (as with ionic compounds)
2. In complex: ligands alphabetically, then metal
3. Anionic ligands end in –o: Cl⁻→chlorido, CN⁻→cyanido, OH⁻→hydroxido, NO₂⁻→nitrito/nitro
4. Neutral ligands: aqua (H₂O), ammine (NH₃), carbonyl (CO), nitrosyl (NO)
5. Metal oxidation state in Roman numerals in parentheses
6. Anionic complex: metal name ends in –ate (cuprate, ferrate, plumbate)

**Examples:**
- [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ → hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride
- K₄[Fe(CN)₆] → potassium hexacyanoferrate(II)
- [Cu(en)₂]SO₄ → bis(ethylenediamine)copper(II) sulfate

## Isomerism

### Structural Isomerism
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Ionisation | Different ions in/outside sphere | [Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄ vs [Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br |
| Linkage | Ambidentate ligand attaches differently | –NO₂ (N-bonded) vs –ONO (O-bonded) |
| Coordination | Ligands distributed differently between two metal centers | — |

### Stereoisomerism
- **Geometric (cis-trans):** square planar (4-coord) and octahedral (6-coord) complexes
  - [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]: cis = cisplatin (anticancer), trans = transplatin (inactive)
- **Optical:** non-superimposable mirror images (enantiomers); requires no plane of symmetry
  - [Co(en)₃]³⁺ is optically active (3 bidentate ligands)

## Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

Ligands as point charges repel d orbitals. Degenerate d orbitals split in field.

### Octahedral field:
```
         eg (dx²-y², dz²) — higher energy (+0.6 Δo)
─────────────────────────────
         t₂g (dxy, dxz, dyz) — lower energy (–0.4 Δo)
```
Δo = crystal field splitting energy

### Square planar field: four ligands, larger splitting than octahedral

### Tetrahedral field: Δt ≈ 4/9 Δo (smaller splitting, usually high spin)

### High spin vs Low spin (octahedral):
- **Strong field ligands** (large Δo → e⁻ pair up) → **low spin**
- **Weak field ligands** (small Δo → e⁻ fill each d before pairing) → **high spin**

**Spectrochemical series (weak → strong field):**
I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < C₂O₄²⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en < NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ < CO

## Practice Questions
1. Name: [Cr(NH₃)₄Cl₂]Cl
2. What is the coordination number of Fe in [Fe(C₂O₄)₃]³⁻?
3. Why does CN⁻ produce low-spin complexes?
4. Does [Co(en)₃]³⁺ show optical isomerism? Why?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. Tetraamminedichloridochromium(III) chloride
> 2. CN = 6 (each oxalate is bidentate, 3 × 2 = 6 donor atoms)
> 3. CN⁻ is a strong-field ligand (high Δo) → electrons pair in t₂g → low spin
> 4. Yes — three bidentate en ligands create a propeller-like arrangement with no plane of symmetry → chiral → optical isomers
