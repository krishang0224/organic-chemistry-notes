# 5.1 SN1 & SN2 Mechanisms

> [!tip] One Line
> Two pathways for nucleophilic substitution — SN2 is one-step (backside attack), SN1 is two-step (via carbocation).

---

## SN2 — Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

**One step, concerted.** Nucleophile attacks from back as leaving group leaves simultaneously.

```
Nu:⁻ + R–X → Nu–R + X⁻
```

**Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]** (both involved in rate-determining step)

**Key features:**
- Backside attack → **Walden inversion** (stereochemistry inverts — like umbrella flipping)
- Favoured by: **1° substrate** (less steric hindrance), strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, acetone)
- Order: CH₃X > 1° > 2° >> 3° (3° essentially doesn't do SN2)

## SN1 — Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

**Two steps.** Leaving group leaves first → carbocation → nucleophile attacks.

```
Step 1: R–X → R⁺ + X⁻     (slow, rate-determining)
Step 2: R⁺ + Nu:⁻ → R–Nu  (fast)
```

**Rate = k[substrate]** (only substrate in rate-determining step)

**Key features:**
- Forms **carbocation** → **racemization** (nucleophile attacks from both sides)
- Favoured by: **3° substrate** (stable carbocation), weak nucleophile, polar protic solvent (water, ethanol)
- Order: 3° > 2° >> 1° (methyl essentially never does SN1)

---

## Comparison Table

| Feature | SN1 | SN2 |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Steps | 2 | 1 |
| Rate law | k[RX] | k[RX][Nu] |
| Substrate | 3° best | 1° best |
| Intermediate | Carbocation | None (transition state) |
| Stereochemistry | Racemization | Inversion |
| Solvent | Polar protic | Polar aprotic |
| Nucleophile | Weak ok | Strong needed |

---

## Leaving Group Ability
I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (better leaving = weaker base = more stable anion)

## Nucleophilicity (polar protic solvents)
I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (same order — larger ion, less solvated)

## Practice Questions
1. Which substrate undergoes SN2 fastest: CH₃Br, (CH₃)₂CHBr, (CH₃)₃CBr?
2. (CH₃)₃CBr + H₂O → ? (mechanism?)
3. Why does SN2 give inversion of configuration?
4. Why do polar aprotic solvents favour SN2?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. CH₃Br (methyl — least steric hindrance for backside attack)
> 2. (CH₃)₃COH via SN1 — forms stable 3° carbocation, water is weak nucleophile, protic solvent
> 3. Nucleophile must approach from back (180° to leaving group) → bonds invert like umbrella
> 4. They don't solvate the nucleophile — nucleophile stays "naked" and more reactive
