# 5.2 Elimination Reactions

> [!tip] One Line
> Elimination = removal of H and X from adjacent carbons to form C=C. Competes with substitution.

---

## E2 — Bimolecular Elimination (one step)
```
Base + H–C–C–X → Base–H + C=C + X⁻
```
**Rate = k[substrate][base]**
- Anti-periplanar geometry required (H and X must be 180° apart)
- Strong bulky base, high temperature favours E2
- **Saytzeff's Rule:** Major product = more substituted alkene (more stable)

## E1 — Unimolecular Elimination (two steps)
```
Step 1: R–X → R⁺ + X⁻    (slow)
Step 2: Base removes H⁺ → alkene
```
**Rate = k[substrate]**
- Same first step as SN1
- Follows Saytzeff's rule
- Competes with SN1

## Saytzeff's Rule
When elimination can give two different alkenes, the **more substituted** alkene is the major product.
```
CH₃CH₂CHBrCH₃ + KOH → CH₃CH=CHCH₃ (major) + CH₃CH₂CH=CH₂ (minor)
                         (more substituted)      (less substituted)
```

## Hofmann's Rule (exception)
With **bulky base** (e.g., KO–C(CH₃)₃), less substituted alkene is major product (steric reasons).

---

## When SN vs E?

| Condition | Favours |
|-----------|---------|
| Strong nucleophile, low temp, 1° substrate | SN2 |
| Weak nucleophile, 3° substrate, protic solvent | SN1 |
| Strong base, high temp | E2 |
| 3° substrate, weak base, high temp | E1 |

---

## Practice Questions
1. CH₃CHBrCH₃ + KOH (alc.) → major product?
2. (CH₃)₃CBr + KOH (alc., hot) → ?
3. Why does high temperature favour elimination over substitution?

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. CH₃CH=CH₂ + CH₂=CHCH₃ → propene (major by Saytzeff, more substituted)... wait both are same. Propene is the only alkene possible.
> 2. (CH₃)₂C=CH₂ (2-methylpropene) — E2, Saytzeff product
> 3. Elimination is endothermic (entropy increases — 1 molecule → 2); higher temp thermodynamically favours it
