# 2.3 Functional Groups

> [!tip] One Line
> A functional group is the reactive part of an organic molecule — it determines what reactions the compound undergoes and most of its physical properties.

## Understanding Each Group

### Haloalkanes (–X)
- X = F, Cl, Br, I (halogen)
- C–X bond is polar (C⁺–X⁻) because X is more electronegative
- Good leaving groups (especially Br and I)
- Key reactions: SN1, SN2, elimination

### Alcohols (–OH)
- O–H bond: very polar, forms **hydrogen bonds**
- Higher boiling point than corresponding alkanes
- Can be 1°, 2°, 3° depending on which C the –OH is on
- Reactions: oxidation (1° → aldehyde → acid), dehydration, esterification

### Phenol (–OH on benzene)
- –OH directly on benzene ring
- More acidic than alcohol (pKa ~10 vs ~16 for alcohol)
- Why? Phenoxide ion is stabilized by resonance with the ring

### Aldehydes (–CHO)
- Carbonyl group (C=O) at the **end** of chain
- C of CHO is always C1 → no locant needed
- Highly reactive: nucleophilic addition, oxidation
- Can be oxidized to carboxylic acid (tollens, fehling tests)

### Ketones (C=O)
- Carbonyl group in the **middle** of chain (not at end)
- Less reactive than aldehydes (steric hindrance)
- Cannot be oxidized easily (no H on carbonyl carbon)

### Carboxylic Acids (–COOH)
- Most acidic functional group among common ones
- Can form hydrogen bonds in two directions → very high boiling points
- React to form esters, amides, salts

### Amines (–NH₂)
- N with lone pair → acts as a **base** (accepts H⁺)
- Can be 1°, 2°, 3° depending on how many C's are attached to N
- Smell: fishy odor

## Priority Order (Critical for IUPAC)

When a molecule has multiple functional groups, the **highest priority** group gets the suffix. Everything else becomes a prefix.

```
–COOH > –CHO > C=O > –OH > –NH₂ > C=C > C≡C > –X
(acid)  (aldeh) (keto) (alc) (amine)  (ene)  (yne) (halo)
```

### Example
Compound: HOCH₂–CH₂–COOH
- Has –OH and –COOH
- –COOH has higher priority → suffix = -oic acid
- –OH becomes prefix = hydroxy-
- Name: **3-hydroxypropanoic acid**

## Homologous Series

A **homologous series** is a family of compounds with:
- Same functional group
- Same general formula
- Each member differs by –CH₂– (14 mass units) from the next
- Gradually changing physical properties (boiling point increases up the series)
- Similar chemical properties

**Example — Alcohols:**
```
Methanol    CH₃OH       bp 65°C
Ethanol     C₂H₅OH      bp 78°C
Propanol    C₃H₇OH      bp 97°C
Butanol     C₄H₉OH      bp 117°C
(Each +CH₂, +14 mass units, +~20°C bp)
```

## Isomerism from Functional Groups

Same molecular formula can have different functional groups:

**C₃H₆O can be:**
- Propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO) — aldehyde
- Propan-2-one (CH₃COCH₃) — ketone
- Both have DoU = 1, same formula — this is **functional group isomerism**

## Practice Questions

1. Identify all functional groups in: HOCH₂–CH₂–NH₂–COOH (is this possible? Draw and discuss)

2. Which has a higher boiling point: pentan-1-ol or pentane? Why?

3. Arrange in order of decreasing acidity: phenol, ethanol, ethanoic acid

4. A compound C₃H₆O has DoU=1. Give two possible functional group isomers.

5. Why can't an aldehyde and a ketone have the same carbon number as the simplest example? (Hint: think about CH₂O)

> [!question]- Answers
> 1. Three groups: –OH (alcohol), –NH₂ (amine), –COOH (acid). Priority: –COOH > –OH > –NH₂
> 2. Pentan-1-ol — due to intermolecular hydrogen bonding (O–H...O), pentane has only weak van der Waals forces
> 3. Ethanoic acid (pKa~5) > phenol (pKa~10) > ethanol (pKa~16) — more acidic = lower pKa
> 4. Propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO) and propan-2-one (CH₃COCH₃)
> 5. Formaldehyde HCHO is both (it's an aldehyde with formula CH₂O — ketone needs at least 3 carbons: CH₃COCH₃)
